Short Summary
When you cannot factor polynomials, such as $$\color{#04afff}{x^2+8x+2}$$ You will have to utilize the quadratic formula to solve for the zeros of the function. The quadratic formula can be derived from the polynomial, $$\color{#04afff}{ax^2+bx+c}$$ But, it would be better for students to just memorize it for now. The formula for the quadratic formula is as follows: $$\color{#04afff}{x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}}$$ To use the quadratic formula, you need to first identify where the variables of the formula are in your polynomial. For example, the provided example has an a value of 1, b value of 8, and a c value of 2. Which is found through referencing with the formula that the quadratic formula is dervied from to find out what coefficients are assigned to what value. $$\color{#04afff}{x = \frac{-8 + \sqrt{8^2-4(1)(2)}}{2(1)}\\\\ x = \frac{-8 - \sqrt{8^2-4(1)(2)}}{2(1)}}$$ As seen above, the plus and minus sign results in you needing to evaulate the formula twice, with one with a negative sign and one with a positive one. $$\color{#04afff}{x=-4+\sqrt{14},\, x=-4-\sqrt{14}}$$ These two values are the zeros of the polynomial provided in this example, which can be double checked with a graph or plugged into the original equation to see if it evaluates to 0.
